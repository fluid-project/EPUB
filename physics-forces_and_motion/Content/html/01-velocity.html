Chapter 1 - Velocity

The following is an adaptation of the original. Source: http://www.ck12.org/physics/Velocity/lesson/Velocity/
Students will learn how analyze and solve problems in two dimensions containing velocity vectors but no acceleration.

Key Equations

  • v x = v cos θ The x component of velocity equals the product of the velocity times cos theta, where theta is the angle between the velocity vector and the horizontal.
  • v y = v sin θ The y component of velocity equals the product of velocity times sin theta, where theta is the angle between the velocity vector and the horizontal.
  • Δ x = v x t The change in distance x equals the product of the x component of velocity and time.
  • Δ y = v y t The change in distance y equals the product of the y component of velocity and time.

Guidance

The only equation you need is that displacement in a certain direction equals the component of velocity in that direction multiplied by the time it takes. You'll use this once for the x-direction and once for the y-direction and solve for what is asked.

Example 1

Question: If a river is flowing north at 2 m/s and you swim straight across (i.e. east) at 1.5 m/s, how far up shore will you be from your starting point once you reach the other side? The river is 9 m wide.
Answer: First solve for the time it takes you to reach the other side. Let's let north be the y-direction and the direction across the river be the x-direction.
Δ x = v x t
The change in distance x equals the product of the x component of velocity and time.
9 m =1.5 m/s × t
The river is 9 meters wide, so the change in distance is 9 meters. This is equal to the product of the velocity of swimming straight across at 1.5 meters per second and the total time it takes to travel across the river.
t = 6 s
Solving for time t, it takes 6 seconds to swim straight across a 9 meter wide river at a velocity of 1.5 meters per second.

Time for Practice

Question 1

If a river is flowing south at 4 m/s and you swim straight across (i.e. east) at 2 m/s; admittedly, you're going to drift a bit south. That said, calculate that distance that you drifted south from your starting point. The river is 16 m wide.
Answer: You would have drifted 32 m south of the starting point.

Question 2

If a river is flowing south at 3 m/s and you swim at an angle of 30 degrees north of directly east at 1 m/s, how far did you drift up or down stream from your starting point once you reach the other side? The river is 10 m wide.
Answer: You would have drifted 28.9 m south of starting point.

Question 3

If a river is flowing north at 2 m/s and you can swim at 4 m/s what angle should you swim at such that you arrive directly across the river (i.e. no drift north or south from starting point on other side)? The river is 10 m wide.
Answer: You would swim at a 30 degree angle to arrive directly across the river.

Question 4

If a river is flowing south at 5 m/s and you can swim at 4 m/s maximum, is it possible to arrive directly across? Why or why not?
Answer: No, even if you swim directly north the river will still take you south at 1 m/s.
